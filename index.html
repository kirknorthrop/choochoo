You've probably heard a lot about Open Data. And you've thought "I'd like to get me some of that". And there's quite a big overlap between programming and trains. So let's see what it's all about...

Three main data providers

Transport for London (TfL)

Rail Delivery Group (RDG)

Network Rail (NR)

There's three sources of rail data in the UK.

First up - Transport for London - they have data for Tube, DLR, London Overground, buses and trams.

Then there's two providers for the main rail network - the Rail Delivery Group, which used to be called ATOC, and is better known as National Rail. It's owned by the rail franchise companies and they mainly care about services and fares.

Network Rail, who actually run the infrastructure of the British railways also have feeds - these are more focussed on signalling and trains.

You can spend ages playing with this data and understanding the links between them all.

So. TfL's live data is easy to get hold of, and as it's JSON, it's even easier for us to use in Python. Unfortunately the quality of the data is highly variable. And it often disappears. Or changes format.
I run a website called Up Down London - my friend is a wheelchair user and she wanted a way to find out which lifts are broken to know where she could and couldn't travel. As part of our quest to make this data as accurate as possible, we've had a lot of meetings and discussions with TfL's data team. We've seen some improvement and believe they want to get better, but there's still a long way to go.
